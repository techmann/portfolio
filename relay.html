    • How do you send mobile content friends and family AND keep it separate?

    When I find something on my phone worth sharing, I've never had the right tool to share it the right way. Group texts don't work, as everyone might not know each other I could use an email and bcc it to everyone, but email isn't personal enough. I don't like posting on my Facebook wall for all to see.

    A product didn't exist so I decided to build it

    From ideation to development, I created Relay to solve a problem I knew everyone was running into. You find an article that you want to send separately to some family members, your roommate, and a couple buddies. Instead of sending it in separate texts, just copy the link, preview it, select the contacts, and hit send. Relay will send the article to everyone, and they can't see who else you sent it to.

    This was the first project that ignited my career and fueled my passion to build it as a side project. It was the capstone project of my user experience design bootcamp (BLOC), so I was able to collect valuable feedback from my mentor (an industry professional). As my user experience knowledge and career grew, so did Relay. Without a team to bounce ideas off of, I constantly challenged myself to gather feedback and iterate the experience.

    Gathering data to verify the need

    I sent out a questionnaire to validate my hypothesis: people are sharing mobile content on platforms that don't support sharing internet content separately. As expected, nobody had an answer that solved the UX problem.

    Sample questions

    "I would send the article link in 3 text messages. One to family, one to friend, and on as a group message to my roommates. Possibly only 2 texts if my friend is also friends with my roommates then all three could be put together."

    - Steve, advanced technologist

    The questionnaires gave me the personas

    All of the data I needed for user personas came out of the questionnaire. I selected personas that balanced age, technology skills, and sharing habits. I wanted this to be simple enough for my grandma to use (spoiler alert, she can).

    Early Designs

    Relay 1.0 was a great starting point, but it was trailing in new design trends and the information architecture needed work. I had the process flows drawn out, but I knew it could be more intuitive.

    I was using a hamburger menu in the initial designs to be the center of navigation.

    It was challenging to design where all the buttons would go, including a preview of what you pasted.

    Inspired by Snapchat, I used a similar approach to selecting contacts.

    v2.0

    It's amazing how much my skills developed in all areas of UX from being around amazing talent at PwC. Combine that with new design trends, and Relay 2.0 turned into a product I was proud of. I found inspiration in big headers, a beautiful color gradient, and restructured the architecture into a tabbed bar.

    While the 3 content options are still there (paste, choose a picture, take a picture), each has a live preview already loaded.

    I added in profile pictures, drop shadows, and added a sent relays button from listening to users.

    Selecting contacts got fun in 2.0. Each contact has an editable emoji assigned by you. I also moved the text input to this screen to help the real estate of the content screen.

    Teaching myself Swift

    The extent of my programming skills was HTML & CSS. Learning Swift for me was like telling a little kid to go jump off the high dive, but I had too much passion for this idea to bring the project to a halt. The reward for building something real and bringing a product to life helped power through late nights and seemingly unsolvable errors. A huge benefit was learning the process for how design artifacts are implemeneted into Xcode, so it was valuable to understand best practices for handoffs of an iOS product build.

    Testing, and more testing

    I was constantly testing Relay. I ran a TestFlight through iTunes Connect for version 1.0, and it was clear as day the entire app needed an overhaul. There was performance issues, information architecture problems, and a lack of direction for users on how to get going. There were two things I discovered that can make or break your app, despite how great it might be at the core: onboarding and empty states.

    Onboarding

    I wanted to have just the right amount of onboarding for a new user. What I came to learn is that the average user is smarter than you think, and more impatient than you think. It's important to show some tips to get started, but leave some curiosity and hidden gems for them to find.

    It took countless iterations of finding the right content and balance. I discovered many don't bother with it, but if they do, you don't have much time. After studying what users needed the most, I settled on getting users to Explore for content to relay, finding friends, and pointing them to the tutorial if they need help later on.

    Empty states

    It wasn't until development was underway that I realized how important empty states were to a user. As I started to work through the process of a new user signing up, it was unclear whether a screen hadn't loaded yet or if something went wrong.

    I made sure that if something was supposed to be empty, it was obvious to the user. As shown in this example, it was very important to let Relay users know they didn't have any friends yet, but they were only a small step away from adding them.
